February Festivals

1. Maha Shivaratri

Maha Shivaratri, translating to "The Great Night of Shiva," is a major Hindu festival celebrated in honor of Lord Shiva. Unlike many Hindu festivals which are celebrated during the day, this is a nocturnal festival. It marks the night when Lord Shiva performs the heavenly dance of creation, preservation, and destruction, and is also believed to be the night of his marriage to Goddess Parvati. Devotees observe a strict fast (vrat) and keep a vigil (jaagran) all night, meditating on ethics and virtues such as self-restraint and forgiveness. Temples are thronged with worshipers offering "Bel Patra" (wood apple leaves), milk, and water to the Shiva Lingam. The festival emphasizes overcoming darkness and ignorance in one's life and the world, symbolizing a spiritual awakening.

2. Goa Carnival

A legacy of Portuguese colonial rule, the Goa Carnival is a vibrant, three-to-four-day festival that typically takes place in February, leading up to Lent. It is one of the few traditional carnivals in Asia and is unique to Goa. The festivities are presided over by "King Momo," a character who declares the decree to "eat, drink, and be merry." The streets of Panaji, Margao, Vasco, and Mapusa come alive with colossal parades featuring colorful floats, masked dancers, and live bands performing music ranging from Konkani folk to Western pop. It is a cultural spectacle that transcends religious boundaries, showcasing Goa's rich heritage, fun-loving spirit, and community harmony. Tourists from all over the world flock to the state to witness this explosion of color and joy.

3. Taj Mahotsav

Held annually in Agra near the eastern gate of the Taj Mahal, the Taj Mahotsav is a 10-day cultural extravaganza that usually takes place in February. It is a celebration of India’s rich arts, crafts, culture, cuisine, and dance. Organized by the Department of Tourism, the festival provides a platform for artisans from across India to display their exquisite works, including wood carvings from Saharanpur, brass wares from Moradabad, handmade carpets from Bhadohi, and pottery from Khurja. The festival begins with a spectacular procession reminiscent of the Mughal era, featuring bedecked elephants and camels. Folk artists perform traditional dances and music, turning the area into a mini-India. It serves as a significant event for preserving traditional Indian craftsmanship and promoting tourism.
