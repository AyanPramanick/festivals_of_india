June Festivals

1. Rath Yatra (Chariot Festival)

While predominantly associated with Puri in Odisha, the Rath Yatra is celebrated across India in June or July. It involves the public procession of the deities Jagannath (Lord of the Universe), Balabhadra, and Subhadra in three gigantic, distinctively decorated wooden chariots. The festival commemorates the deities' annual visit to their aunt's temple, the Gundicha Temple. The act of pulling the chariots by ropes is considered an act of great devotion and is believed to wash away sins. Millions of devotees flock to Puri to witness this spectacle. The chariots are architectural marvels, constructed anew every year. The festival is unique because it is one of the few occasions where the temple deities are brought out of the sanctum sanctorum to mingle with the masses, symbolizing equality and accessibility of the divine to all.

2. Muharram Festival

Muharram is the first month of the Islamic lunar calendar, a sacred time marked by reflection, mourning, and the observance of the Islamic New Year, especially significant for Shia Muslims as a period to commemorate the martyrdom of Imam Hussain at the Battle of Karbala (Day of Ashura), while many Sunni Muslims observe the day by fasting to remember Moses' victory over Pharaoh. Rituals include mourning processions with black attire and self-flagellation (controversial) for Shias, and voluntary fasting for Sunnis, all centered on devotion and spiritual renewal, with events like Ashura (the 10th day) being key.

3. Ganga Dussehra

OCelebrated in June, Ganga Dussehra marks the day the holy River Ganges descended from heaven to earth to purge the cursed souls of Bhagiratha's ancestors. The festival lasts for ten days and is observed with great fervor in states like Uttarakhand, Uttar Pradesh, and Bihar, particularly in cities like Haridwar, Rishikesh, and Varanasi. Devotees flock to the riverbanks to take a holy dip, believing that the water on this day has the power to cleanse ten types of sins. In the evening, the river is worshipped with "Aarti," and thousands of earthen lamps (diyas) are floated on the water, creating a mesmerizing sight. It is a festival that underscores the ecological and spiritual importance of the river in Indian culture
